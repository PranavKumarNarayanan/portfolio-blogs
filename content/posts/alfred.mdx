Today, I'm very elated to introduce our new product, my first complete project after joining university. **Alfred**. 

This is one of those projects where you have been having the idea for a long time, but didn't really have much faith in it, until you eventually did. 

There are two reasons for why I'm writing this blog. One, is for it to be a self-promotion to my project, and the other is to document my learning.
## The "Innovation" Dogma
Right from the very beginning, both in my life and in my university, we have been partly forced, partly encouraged to do more innovation activities. And this is not personal, many if not all people face this. 

In our university, lot of activities are centered towards "student innovation". Activities that would encourage students to make a change in the industry. But all of them misses a very important point, and that leads a lot of students in the wrong path. You see, the biggest problem with this approach is that, a false narrative is created in the minds of the students that creating something "completely new" and "ground-breaking" is the only way to move forward. Many hackathons also follow the same narrative and push this idea in the minds of students even further. But the reality is far from that. Sure, innovation is a mandatory element if you want to sell your product. But people confuse the terms "innovative" and "unique" more often than one would expect in this field.

I went through the landing pages (GitHub repositories instead, for open-source projects) of multiple fast growing and successful startups and found one common element in them. 

1. None of them created a completely new product
2. All of them made a product that would make a specific task easier.

One good example would be WorkOS (check them out [here](https://workos.com/)). Once you go through their product description, you can get an idea on what I'm talking about. 

Their entire product is to provide authentication to web applications. That's it. There is nothing innovative there, nothing fancy, nothing gimmicky. They didn't create any authentication protocols, they didn't shove up "AI" and other fancy terminologies up our faces. 

All they did was to beautifully integrate all the types of sign in methods and create a dashboard for them, making it easier to manage for the IT admins of their clients. 

And this is not the only example.

The point I'm trying to make here is, for a project/startup/idea to be successful, you don't need a completely new and innovative product. All you need to do is to identify the pain-points in the industry of your interest, take existing solutions, and make them easily accessible. The actual market out there wants easy, clean, and maintainable solutions than new, and experimental ones. 

## Alfred
Using the philosophy I learnt by looking at these companies, me, and my awesome team set out to create something that would solve a simple yet crucial problem for developers. Something that we wish we had. So we created Alfred. 

Alfred is an end-to-end AI workflow system, designed to bring the perks of having an AI system to your Project management system. Essentially, we want you to be able to perform tasks that doesn't actually deserve your time, but still is crucial for the project. 

Things like setting up your environment variables, formatting your code, rephrasing, and writing documentation. Everything that doesn't require your time and expertise to be spent but yet is of paramount importance to the project. 

Our idea is simple, you assign the task in your project management system, and the AI takes from there. One might think that this does nothing different from just entering the prompt in your LLM. But that's the point. We don't aim to create something new or innovative. We want to take one problem and solve it "elegantly". 

Every employee regardless of their place of work has some sort of project management system; Jira, Openprojects and Monday.com are a few examples. And naturally tasks are assigned to them, or by them. Our mission is straightforward. Start from there, build boilerplates for your tasks. Create templates and codebases for your projects and let you take it from there. 

## Where Is It Right Now?
Right now, Alfred is in a very primitive state. The repository exists (you can find it [here](https://github.com/Dreamteam-Nagercoil/alfred)). It is not in a state to be even called pre-alpha. You could say it's in ideation stage, if you may. A very primitive prototype has been built and we are working on it. 

I have a lot of plans for it and I see a future for it. And I hope this turns out to be better than what it is right now, and grow even further.
